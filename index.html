National Park Service Redesign

The National Park Service is an agency of the United States Government under the Department of the Interior that oversees over 400 national parks, national monuments, and natural, historical and recreational properties within the United States. Its website aims to educate users about the National Park Service and help them discover America's natural landscapes.

The current user experience of the National Park Service is confusing and outdated, and it’s navigation is difficult to understand. The UX team redesigned the website to focus on a more stylized and clean UI and a cohesive navigation.

Project Overview

In this research project, the UX Team conducted a Heuristic Evaluation of the existing National Park Service website and completed 6 usability tests to capture users’ pain points on the NPS site. 

Based on user findings, the team prioritized simplifying the content on the homepage and updating the top navigation for a more intuitive experience. The team created a RWD prototype and had 3 rounds of iterations based on usability tests.

Research

Heuristic Evaluation

To get a gauge on the current usability of the National Park Service Site, the UX team conducted it’s own Heuristic Evaluation , going through its navigation and internal park sites to evaluate the cohesiveness of its branding and aesthetics, the intuitiveness of its navigation– including the search bar, and the simplicity of its content.

The team found that the site had little brand consistency, with no clear color scheme and a near unusable search bar, with no way to filter or sort when searching for something, such when looking for information about a National Park.

Interviews

Based on user findings, the team uncovered that users struggled with navigating the site, as the search feature had no way of sorting or filtering system, and many links under the header navigation was misleading or had multiple ways to find the same information but the page itself was laid out differently. With this discovery, the team came up with the problem statement to build towards.

"How might we simplify the navigation of the site and strengthen brand recognition so that users can confidently find parks to visit and activities to explore?"

The UX Team then conducted usability tests with 6 participants, who were instructed to find information on the NPS website using the search bar, top navigation, and home navigation.

Based on user findings, the team created a 2x2 matrix that took user wants and pain points and prioritized the top 2 features: simplifying the content on the homepage and giving users an easier way to search by park and activity.

Ideation

Card Sorting

The current layout (left) of the National Park’s top navigation is disjointed and both users and the UX team found that many of the pages did not intuitively fit into each category it was under. The team conducted a card sort of the top navigation, adding new categories to better point users to where they wanted to go (right).

Prototyping

Wireframes

The layout of the low-fidelity prototype was loyal to the existing National Park Service site, with a blocky black and white explore dropdown menu, with the main changes being to simplifying the top navigation and adding a carousel for the Featured Stories. Horizontal bars were used to highlight special features like National Park of the Month and the National Park Foundation, but the UX team found it didn’t allow for either feature to be highlighted.

Desktop Prototype

Style Guide

The style inspiration for the new UI was the WPA National Park posters from the 1930s, so colors were picked from the posters and logo, with typography similarly going for a vintage but clean aesthetic. This was chosen to solidify the National Park Service’s branding as exemplifying the classic American Outdoors.

Usability Testing & Findings

High Fidelity Prototype

Based on initial feedback from the 5 second usability tests, the header menu was made solidly black to avoid any overlap of text when the page has scrolled down. When implementing the style guide, the tan bar was included to highlight special features of the site, such as a National Park of the Month.

Usability Tests & Revisions

Based on 6 user tests, the team gained insight that it was difficult to tell that the cards could be scrolled with just having one card visible at a time. They also expected the Careers page to be in the footer menu instead of the top navigation. The team made iterations especially to the mobile prototype, adding a peek-a-boo functionality for the cards and moving the Careers page to the footer.
